01  The problem

Everyone ran the same experiment. Then the lights came down.

Hand developers AI tools and throughput jumps 60%+. Then the curve bends, the next cohort gains less, and buying more tools barely moves the line.

The ceiling is structural — not a tooling problem.

The diagnosis · wall one

Tools speed the middle, not the ends.

Tools speed up one part of building software — writing the code. Speeding up the middle just moves the constraint to the two ends tools don't touch — deciding what to build, and keeping the system healthy.

A faster middle makes both worse.

wall two

You cannot automate vagueness.

My mother could cook for forty from "salt as needed, cook till it's done." Hand that to someone who's never stood at the stove and you don't get the feast faster — you get confusion faster.

An agent is the new cook. It amplifies whatever process it's dropped into.

wall three

Individual gains don't compound.

A tool makes one person faster. Twenty engineers 40% faster in twenty directions isn't a 40% faster org.

It's twenty faster silos and a coordination problem that eats the gain.

The Product Development Operating System5 / 10

02  The fix

An operating system, not more tools.

A real OS schedules, allocates, enforces contracts, abstracts the machine — and exposes governors you tune to context.

Raw processors aren't a computer. Fast engineers with tools aren't an operating system.

04  The honest part

The constraint doesn't vanish. It moves.

Automate both ends and the bottleneck relocates — to the clarity / SME node and the human-review queue.

That's the real design problem: subordinate everything to it, then raise it. Leverage is capped by it.

05  The metric

Leverage per engineer.

customer-impacting outcome ÷ humans
  • Outcome, not output — measured after ship.
  • Humans only; agents counted separately.
  • Never reported alone — paired with a quality guardrail.
  • An org-level trend, never a target. No "2×" to promise.
