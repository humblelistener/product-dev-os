White Paper · Engineering Leadership in the Agentic Era

The Product Development Operating System

AI coding tools gave every engineering organisation a one-time jump in output. Then the curve flattened. The next gain isn't another tool — it's structural. Here is the structure, where it breaks, and where the constraint goes next.

By Karthik Padmanabhan June 2026 · ~28 min read

Also available: Deck · Infographic · Blog post

I have watched this happen in enough engineering teams now to stop calling it luck. You hand your developers the new AI assistants — the completion, the chat, the agent in the editor — and for a few months the mood is festival: throughput climbs, the dashboards light up, everyone is pleased. The numbers are real and they are large. In our own data, pull requests per engineer rose by more than 60% inside two quarters, and the share of code with AI somewhere in its lineage passed 80%. Versions of this result are being reported everywhere, by organisations that look nothing alike.

Then, quietly, the lights come down. The curve bends; the second derivative goes negative. The first cohort of adopters gets a dramatic lift, the next gets less, and the organisation settles onto a new, higher plateau and stops climbing. Leaders respond the way they were trained to — they buy more tools. A better model. Another seat licence. A new agent in the editor. And the line barely moves.

This paper is about why that plateau is not a tooling problem, what actually breaks through it, and — the part most writing in this space skips — where the constraint moves once you do break through. The short version: the gains from tools are real but bounded, because tools speed up one part of building software — writing the code — while the work that actually constrains you sits at the two ends they don't touch. To go further you stop buying capabilities and start building a system that organises them and tunes them to how your organisation works. You need an operating system for product development. But an operating system does not abolish the constraint; it relocates it to somewhere harder and more human — and the honest design problem is what you do there.

The plateau is not a sign that AI has run out of room. It is a sign that you have automated the easy middle and left the hard ends untouched.

I.The ceiling

Why AI tools plateau

The plateau looks like a single wall, but it is three walls in a row. Each one is enough to stop you on its own. Together they explain why the spend keeps rising while the curve keeps flattening.

Wall one

Tools speed the middle, not the ends

Software delivery has three stretches. Upstream: deciding what is worth building — turning signals from customers, research, support and operations into a clear, validated piece of work. The middle: writing the code that implements it. Downstream: keeping the resulting system healthy — patched, secure, observable, deployable, fast.

Tools speed up the one part of building software — writing the code — and they are extraordinary at it. But speeding up the middle does not remove the constraint; it moves it, intact, to the two ends you didn't touch: the ambiguity at the front, and the accumulating weight of maintenance at the back. Worse, a faster middle increases pressure on both ends: more code arrives upstream demanding clearer decisions, and more code lands downstream demanding review, hardening and care.

Wall two

You cannot automate vagueness

The instinct, once you hit the first wall, is to point agents at the ends. Let them do the research. Let them run the upgrades. This works far less well than people expect, and the reason is structural rather than technical.

An agent amplifies the process it is dropped into. My mother could cook for forty people from a recipe that lived only in her head and her wrist — "salt as needed, cook till it is done." Hand that same recipe to someone who has never stood at the stove and you do not get her feast faster; you get confusion faster. An agent is the new cook. Give it a precise recipe — a crisp specification, a clear definition of done, a bounded surface — and it is magnificent. Give it the way most product decisions are actually made — a backlog of one-line tickets, tribal context, a roadmap that is really a wish-list — and it amplifies that. You get ambiguity, faster: the agent produces something plausible, someone has to untangle whether it was the right thing, and the round-trip eats the gain.

This is why the ends resist automation in a way the middle never did. The ends are where the vagueness lives. And vagueness is not a model capability problem you can wait out with the next release — it is a property of the organisation's process. No frontier model fixes an organisation that cannot say precisely what it wants.

Wall three

Individual gains don't compound

Suppose you get past the first two walls for a given engineer — clear work in, fast execution, clean output. You have made one person faster. That is a local optimisation, and local optimisations don't add up on their own.

A tool sits with the individual. It has no view of the portfolio, no opinion about whether this work is the right work, no memory that connects the research that justified a feature to the mission that built it to the operational signal that should feed the next decision. Twenty engineers each running 40% faster in twenty different directions does not give you a 40% faster organisation. It gives you twenty faster silos and a coordination problem that quietly absorbs most of the gain.

Compounding requires a layer that the individual tool cannot provide: something that allocates, sequences, connects and remembers across the whole flow. Without it, every gain is a one-time bump — which is exactly what the plateau is.

Notice that none of the three walls is about model quality. The ceiling is not waiting on a smarter model. It is structural. You have automated the easy middle and left the hard ends — and the connections between them — exactly as they were.

II.The reframe

An operating system, not more tools

If the problem is structural, the answer has to be structural. And there is a precise word for the structure that turns a pile of fast components into a system that does useful work: an operating system.

The analogy is exact, not decorative. A computer's operating system does four things. It schedules work onto scarce resources. It allocates those resources according to priority. It enforces contracts so that components can trust each other and fail safely. And it abstracts the machine so that the people writing applications don't have to think about the hardware underneath. Raw processors and memory are not a computer. The operating system is what makes them one.

There is a fifth property that turns out to matter more than the four, and it is the one almost everyone misses. A real operating system is not a fixed thing — it exposes governors: tunable parameters that adapt its behaviour to the machine it is running on and the load it is carrying. An organisation with a two-day customer feedback loop should not run the same way as one whose feedback loop is two quarters long. The system has to be tunable to context — and, as we will see, it is two such regulators that do most of the work, and one closed control loop that makes them real rather than rhetorical.

Be honest about where the analogy breaks, because the break is the most important part of the paper. An operating system schedules work that arrives already specified — it never co-authors the programs it runs. Ours has to. The hardest, most human act in product development is deciding what is worth building and saying it precisely enough to build, and no OS analogy covers it. Rather than paper over the seam, this paper treats it as the crux: the component that manufactures clarity is where the real difficulty lives, and — as Part IV shows — it is where the constraint relocates the moment everything else is automated.

Raw processors aren't a computer. Fast engineers with AI tools aren't an operating system. The OS is what turns components into a system that compounds — and an honest OS tells you where its own bottleneck moves to.

III.The architecture

What the system actually looks like

Concreteness is the test. It is easy to say "operating system" and mean nothing. Here is a real architecture: two planes connected by a telemetry loop, tuned by two regulators. The flow plane governs how work moves from signal to shipped change. The technology plane is the shared ground every builder — human or agent — actually builds on. The split is the platform-team / stream-aligned-team distinction from Team Topologies, made concrete in software.

The flow plane: knowledge agents

Organisation Knowledge agents sit at the head of the flow. A set of agents continuously turns raw signals — interviews, support tickets, usage data, incident patterns, platform risk, industry movement — into a maintained organisational knowledge base. This is the upstream end that tools never touched, now owned by the system rather than living in people's heads. (It carries a real risk — a knowledge base that ingests the system's own prior outputs can poison itself; Part VII deals with it.)

The flow plane: the buildable backlog agent

The Buildable Backlog agent is the heart of the flow and the most underestimated component in any AI delivery story. Its job is to convert knowledge into a backlog that is actually buildable. It does two things: it classifies every item by its true nature — an experiment (testing a hypothesis), a learning task (reducing uncertainty), a build (we know what to make), or a bug (something is broken) — and it manufactures clarity.

"Manufactures clarity" is the phrase a sharp reader should refuse to accept on faith, because it is the exact thing Wall Two says you cannot automate. So here is the mechanism, not the word. Clarity is not a feeling; it is a checklist. An item is buildable only when it satisfies an explicit, inspectable rubric:

The clarity rubric — an item is "buildable" only when
  • the problem and the affected customer or segment are stated;
  • a success signal is named — the customer-observable thing that should move;
  • dependencies and affected services are identified;
  • acceptance criteria are written and testable;
  • a risk class is assigned (which sets how the change is reviewed downstream).

The agent runs every item against that rubric and returns one of two verdicts: buildable, or insufficient — escalate. There is no third "ship it and hope" path; that path is the vagueness Wall Two warns about. On insufficient, the agent routes the specific gap to the right consultant: a retrievable gap (a fact that exists somewhere) goes to an AI consultant-agent that mines the knowledge base; a judgement gap (a decision only an expert can make) goes to a human SME, in a structured plan-mode interaction that proposes options and records the decision. This is dual-track product discovery (Cagan; Torres) and a "definition of ready" — but enforced by a system with a verdict, rather than left to a team's discipline.

The honesty test for the whole component is a single number: the bounce-back rate — the proportion of items that passed the gate as "buildable" but had their scope or definition materially change once build started. A low and falling bounce-back rate means clarity is real. A high one means the agent is manufacturing confident nonsense, and you would never know without the metric. That number also turns out to be the sensor for one of the two regulators (Part V).

The flow plane: builders and missions

Product and platform builders — human and agent — take buildable items and run them as missions, deliberately time-boxed to weeks, never a quarter. Fixed time, variable scope, a deliberate appetite per mission: this is Basecamp's Shape Up, and the debt is worth naming plainly. What is different here is upstream of the mission, not inside it — missions are drawn from a system-classified, clarity-gated backlog whose mix is set by a regulator, rather than chosen at a betting table by hand. A mission is also not a hand-off to nobody: the stream-aligned team that runs it owns the service afterwards. Ownership after the time-box is a first-class question, returned to in Part VII.

The technology plane

Underneath the flow, every builder works in one shared cloud development environment — provisioned in a click, identical everywhere. This is not a convenience feature; it is what makes the rest safe and coherent, and it earns its place three ways.

  1. Fluid movement, one experience. A builder moving between systems gets the same environment every time — no per-repo setup tax, no drift.
  2. Verifiable autonomy. Toil agents run in the same environment as humans, so a person can step directly into what an agent did and verify it. Autonomy you can inspect is autonomy you can trust.
  3. Safe autonomy. You do not hand an agent a high-privilege developer laptop. You give it a disposable, controlled sandbox. The shared environment is what makes broad autonomous permission defensible at all — the blast radius is bounded by construction. (Bounded, not eliminated; a triggerable agent is an attack surface, and Part VII treats it as one.)

On this plane live the toil and BAU agents — the downstream end, automated. They are scheduled, run as routines, triggered from chat against a ticket, or fired by a vulnerability or incident alert. They pick up the low-hanging, high-volume work that otherwise erodes builder time.

The gate, and what happens when it is wrong

The plane carries the control that lets that automation be trusted at scale: a repo-level code-review rubric. A code-review agent applies industry standards — design, coding practice, security — against each repository's own rubric for testing, acceptability, risk and deployment, and it generates test cases to cover the scope of a change. Its verdict is a traffic signal: green merges automatically, amber routes to a human, red stops.

That much is the easy part, and on its own it is dangerous. An agent that writes the code, writes the tests, and approves the merge has no independent oracle anywhere in the loop, and a self-tuning gate that widens "green" by imitating its own past approvals is a ratchet, not a gate — it will optimise for merge velocity and inherit every past mistake. So the gate is specified by its failure semantics, not its happy path:

Failure semantics of the gate
Independent oracles
Green never auto-merges on the review agent's word alone. It clears only behind oracles the agent did not author: the pre-existing regression suite, and a staged canary with automatic rollback on an SLO breach. The agent's generated tests are additive, never the gate.
A hard ceiling
Some change classes are never green regardless of rubric score — authentication and access control, security-sensitive surfaces, data migrations, and public API contracts. These are always amber at minimum.
Earned autonomy, against ground truth
The rubric does not online-learn from its own approvals. It improves through human-curated examples added on a cadence, and the auto-green envelope widens only when the measured false-green rate — greens that caused a production incident — stays below a set threshold. Autonomy is earned against escaped defects, not granted by imitation.
A named owner
A human owns the gate's policy. A green that ships a regression is that owner's incident, and mean-time-to-revert is tracked as a first-class SLO. An operating system needs operators.

Specified this way, the gate is how toil gets out of valuable builder time without anyone abandoning control. Specified the lazy way — green auto-merges, the gate learns from itself — it is how you ship a confident regression at 2am with no one accountable.

IV.The honest part

Where the constraint goes next

Most writing about AI and engineering stops at "we automated the bottleneck." I have learned to distrust that sentence, because theory of constraints says it is never true. You do not remove a constraint; you move it — squeeze one end of a balloon and the air does not leave, it bulges out the other. Relieve the binding constraint and the system speeds up until it binds somewhere else; and if you are not watching, the new constraint is hiding behind the very thing you just made fast.

So apply that discipline to this architecture honestly. Automate the upstream end with the knowledge and backlog agents. Automate the downstream end with the toil agents and the gate. Where does the constraint land? In two coupled places, and the diagram points straight at them.

The first is the clarity-manufacturing node — the backlog agent and, behind it, the scarce human SME judgement that the insufficient → escalate path depends on. Every item in the system funnels through this one station, and its hardest input is expert attention, which does not scale with compute. Build a system that makes everything else fast and you have built a system that routes all of its flow into the one place a machine cannot fully staff.

The second is the human-review queue — every amber verdict from the gate. A faster middle increases the arrival rate into that queue; the queue's service rate is bounded by human reviewers. Little's Law is not optional: when arrivals outrun service, the queue depth — and therefore the lead time of everything in it — grows without bound. The gate, the thing you built to remove the review bottleneck, becomes the review bottleneck.

Build a system that makes everything else fast, and you have built a system that funnels all of its flow into the two places a machine cannot fully staff: expert judgement, and human review.

This is not a flaw to hide. It is the design problem the rest of the system exists to manage, and naming it is what separates an operating system from a pipeline with good marketing. Once you see that the constraint is clarity-and-review capacity, the job of the architecture becomes clear: subordinate everything to that constraint, and then raise it. You subordinate by never letting the fast middle flood the two queues faster than they can clear — which is exactly what the regulators are for. You raise the constraint two ways: by widening the gate's earned-autonomy envelope so fewer changes need a human at all, and by deepening the knowledge base so the backlog agent resolves more gaps as retrievable rather than escalating them as judgement. Leverage, in the end, is capped by the capacity of the clarity-and-review node. The whole point of the system is to push that cap up deliberately — and to never pretend it isn't there.

V.The regulators

Knobs, or actual control loops?

The regulators are where the system becomes yours rather than a prescription — and they are also where it is easiest to cheat. Call something a "regulator," draw a dial, and you have borrowed the authority of control theory while specifying only a setpoint. A setpoint with no measured feedback is not a regulator; it is a policy with good branding. A real regulator is a closed loop: a controlled variable, a setpoint, a sensor, a comparator, and an actuator that changes the system when the two diverge.

Regulator one
Clarity

Controls how much is specified upstream versus discovered in build — the problem/solution balance. This is continuous-discovery calibration (Cagan, Torres), made a system parameter instead of a team habit.

Regulator two
Throughput

Controls the desired mix across the work classifications — experiment, learning, build, bug. The deliberate ratio between betting, de-risking, building and fixing, held by the system rather than won by the loudest stakeholder.

Here is the clarity regulator specified as a loop, end to end — the test of whether any of this is real:

The clarity regulator, as a closed loop
Controlled variable
The bounce-back rate from Part III — how often work changes definition after build starts. It is the operational signature of how much clarity you are actually manufacturing.
Setpoint
Tied to feedback-cycle length. Short cycle, team close to customers: a higher tolerated bounce-back — it is cheap to learn in-flight, so demand less upfront and let builders co-create with customers. Long cycle, team disconnected: a low tolerated bounce-back — being wrong is expensive, so push solution and experiment definition onto SMEs before a mission starts.
Sensor
Telemetry from the mission sub-system reports actual bounce-back, per classification.
Comparator & actuator
The backlog agent compares actual against setpoint. Above setpoint, it raises the clarity bar — more rubric items mandatory, more SME consultation before "buildable." Below it, it relaxes — fewer gates, more in-flight discovery. The dial is not set by hand each week; it is driven by the error signal.

The throughput regulator is the same shape: its controlled variable is the realised mix of shipped missions against the target mix; its sensor is the classification telemetry; its actuator is the backlog agent's admission control — what it releases to builders next. And here is the part the dial-drawing version always omits: the two regulators fight. They draw on the same scarce resource — expert and builder attention. Raising the clarity bar consumes SME time that bug-fix throughput also wants. Two coupled controllers feeding off shared telemetry and competing for one resource can oscillate or stall. The fix is the ordinary one from control engineering: a shared priority, and damping — you do not re-tune either loop faster than the system can respond, or they chase each other. This is the genuinely hard control problem in the whole design, and it is named here rather than hidden behind a knob.

Telemetry: the loop, not the logging

This is also what telemetry is for, and why "every stage emits telemetry" is a claim that has to earn itself. Emitting events and ingesting them is observability; it is not control. Control needs a closed loop where something acts on the error. One worked example, end to end: rising downstream incident telemetry on a set of services (sensor) raises the risk weight the backlog classifier assigns them (comparator); the throughput regulator shifts the target mix toward bug and learning work for those services (actuator); the incident rate falls in the next mission cohort (the loop closes, measured). That is a feedback loop. A dashboard pointed at the front of a pipeline is not.

VI.The metric

Leverage per engineer — done properly

You cannot manage toward a plateau you cannot see, and most engineering dashboards are built to hide this one. They measure output: commits, pull requests, story points, deploys. Output is exactly what AI tools inflated, which is why output metrics looked spectacular right up until the curve bent — and then kept looking fine while the organisation stopped improving.

The metric that exposes the plateau is leverage per engineer. But stated carelessly it commits the very sin this section opens by attacking — so it has to be built with the same discipline as the gate, or it becomes a new vanity number that gets gamed inside a quarter. Three rules make it honest:

  1. The numerator is an outcome, not a change count. Not "customer-impacting change" as a self-declared label at merge — that is a feature flag for cherry-picking. Tie it to a customer-observable signal on the surface that changed: adoption, retention, task-success, or the value metric stated in the clarity rubric, measured after ship. A change that ships and moves nothing a customer does is not counted, however large the diff.
  2. The denominator is humans, and only humans. Agents are tracked on a separate line — their cost and their leverage — never folded into "team size." Otherwise the ratio is gamed by reclassifying people as agents, and, worse, it penalises exactly the platform investment this paper argues for, because platform engineers amplify others rather than shipping customer-facing change themselves.
  3. Never report it alone. Pair it with a stability counter-metric — change-failure rate, reliability — the way DORA pairs throughput with change-fail and SPACE was built to stop single-metric tyranny. Leverage that rises while failure rate rises is churn wearing a halo, not leverage.

And use it as an organisation-level trend, never a team-level target. The moment it becomes a target, Goodhart's law takes it: it stops measuring leverage and starts measuring whatever is easiest to do that makes the number go up.

What about the destination — the "2× per engineer" that a pitch deck wants here? There isn't an honest one to promise, and a paper whose first act is teaching you to distrust headline numbers has no business handing you one for its own product. The ceiling on leverage is set by the clarity-and-review constraint of Part IV. The gain is whatever raising that constraint allows — a hypothesis this metric exists to test, not a number to put on a slide.

Output is what tools inflate. Leverage per engineer — outcome-anchored, human-denominated, paired with a quality guardrail — is what an operating system compounds. The number is a hypothesis to test, not a destination to promise.

VII.What's still hard

The problems this doesn't solve

An operating system that pretends to have no open problems is the surest sign it has never run. Five are real, and naming them is part of the design, not an apology for it.

Evaluation and drift

Every agent here — knowledge, backlog, review, toil — needs an offline evaluation suite and a regression gate, and every prompt or model change is a deployment that must clear it. Without evals, "the rubric learns" is unfalsifiable and "the agent manufactures clarity" is unmeasurable. Model version bumps and prompt edits drift behaviour silently; the answer is the boring engineering one — versioning, canaries, rollback — applied to agents as rigorously as to services.

Model collapse on the knowledge base

The knowledge base feeds the backlog agent and is fed, in part, by the system's own outputs. Without provenance on every fact and a human-curated ground-truth core, it can quietly ingest its own prior conclusions and converge on confident, self-referential wrongness. Telemetry must carry provenance, and the knowledge base needs a notion of which facts are observed versus inferred.

Security of autonomous agents

A sandboxed agent with merge rights, triggered by tickets and alerts, is a prompt-injection and privilege-escalation surface. A crafted ticket or alert payload can drive a toil agent to act. The sandbox bounds the blast radius; it does not sanitise the input. This needs the usual discipline made explicit for agents — input sanitisation, least-privilege tool scopes, secrets kept out of reach — because "safe autonomy" is a property you engineer, not a property of being in a sandbox.

The economics of supervision

The clarity-escalation path and the amber-review queue are the cost centre and, per Part IV, the constraint. If a large fraction of items escalate or go amber, you have relocated toil into supervision, not removed it — and the leverage metric must count that supervision as human time or it will report a rising number while engineers quietly become full-time agent babysitters. The plateau, wearing a new hat.

Operability and ownership

An operating system needs operators. Who runs the agents, the shared environment, and the gate at 2am when they fail? The Team Topologies answer is the right one: the platform is a product with users — the builders — and it has its own backlog, on-call and golden paths. Missions are run by stream-aligned teams who own the service after the time-box ends. A mission that hands off to nobody is how you accumulate the very maintenance debt the toil agents were meant to retire.

What this stands on, and the narrow claim. None of the parts are unprecedented. The clarity regulator is continuous discovery and dual-track development (Marty Cagan; Teresa Torres). Missions are Shape Up (Basecamp) — fixed time, variable scope, an appetite. The two-plane split is Team Topologies (platform and stream-aligned teams). The metric discipline is DORA and SPACE. The single, falsifiable claim of this paper is narrower than any of them: that the calibration of clarity to feedback-cycle length, and the work mix across classifications, can be made system-enforced parameters with measured feedback loops rather than team rituals — and that doing so is what lets one operating system run correctly across organisations that look nothing alike. Everything else is assembly.

VIII.Close

The line moves

You do not need to build this whole system to find out whether you are hitting the ceiling. Measure two numbers first. Your bounce-back rate — how often work changes definition after build starts — tells you how much clarity you are actually manufacturing today. Your review-queue depth — how long a change waits for human judgement — tells you where your constraint already sits. Those two numbers locate your constraint before you spend a dollar on architecture, and they are the same two numbers the system uses to regulate itself. Start by measuring them. If they are healthy, your plateau is somewhere else and this is not your paper. If they are not, you have found your bottleneck, and no additional tool will move it.

It is tempting to frame the agentic era as engineers typing faster. That framing is the plateau in disguise — it keeps your attention on the middle — the one part tools already made fast — and it sends you back to the store for another tool every time the curve bends.

The real shift is quieter and larger. The line of responsibility between people and their machines is moving. The work that does not require human judgement — the patching, the grooming, the routine review, the maintenance that grows without end — moves to agents on the technology plane. What stays with people is the part that was always the point, and that the architecture is honest enough to admit it cannot automate: deciding what is worth building, co-creating clarity, and judging whether what got built is right. An operating system for product development is the structure that moves that line deliberately, tunes itself to your context, and is candid about where its own bottleneck now lives.

Tools gave you a bump. The operating system is how you build a slope. The organisations that understand the difference — and that are willing to stare at their new constraint instead of buying around it — will spend the next few years pulling away from the ones still waiting for a better model to do it for them.
