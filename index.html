White Paper · Engineering Leadership in the Agentic Era

The Product Development Operating System

AI coding tools gave every engineering organisation a one-time jump in output. Then the curve flattened. The next gain isn't another tool — it's structural.

By Karthik Padmanabhan June 2026 · ~15 min read

Almost every engineering organisation has now run the same experiment. Hand developers AI assistants — completion, chat, agentic coding in the editor — and watch throughput climb. The numbers are real and they are large. In our own data, pull requests per engineer rose by more than 60% inside two quarters, and the share of code with AI somewhere in its lineage passed 80%. Versions of this result are being reported everywhere, by organisations that look nothing alike.

Then something less discussed happens. The curve bends. The second derivative goes negative. The first cohort of adopters gets a dramatic lift; the next gets less; the organisation settles onto a new, higher plateau and stops climbing. Leaders respond the way they were trained to respond — they buy more tools. A better model. Another seat licence. A new agent in the IDE. And the line barely moves.

This paper is about why that plateau is not a tooling problem, and what actually breaks through it. The short version: the gains from tools are real but bounded, because tools optimise the one part of software delivery that was never the constraint. To get the next order of magnitude you have to stop buying capabilities and start building a system that organises them. You need an operating system for product development.

The plateau is not a sign that AI has run out of room. It is a sign that you have automated the easy middle and left the hard ends untouched.

I.The ceiling

Why AI tools plateau

The plateau looks like a single wall, but it is three walls in a row. Each one is enough to stop you on its own. Together they explain why the spend keeps rising while the curve keeps flattening.

Wall one

Tools speed the middle, not the ends

Software delivery has three stretches. Upstream: deciding what is worth building — turning signals from customers, research, support and operations into a clear, validated piece of work. The middle: writing the code that implements it. Downstream: keeping the resulting system healthy — patched, secure, observable, deployable, fast.

AI coding tools attack the middle, and they are extraordinary at it. But the middle was rarely the bottleneck. Most organisations were never primarily constrained by typing speed. They were constrained by ambiguity at the front and by the accumulating weight of maintenance at the back. Make the middle ten times faster and you have done something genuinely valuable — and you have also moved the constraint, intact, to the two ends you didn't touch. Worse, a faster middle increases pressure on both ends: more code arrives upstream demanding clearer decisions, and more code lands downstream demanding review, hardening and care.

Wall two

You cannot automate vagueness

The instinct, once you hit the first wall, is to point agents at the ends. Let them do the research. Let them run the upgrades. This works far less well than people expect, and the reason is structural rather than technical.

An agent amplifies the process it is dropped into. Give it a crisp specification, a clear definition of done and a bounded surface, and it performs. Give it the way most product decisions are actually made — a backlog of one-line tickets, tribal context, a roadmap that is really a wish-list — and it amplifies that. You get ambiguity, faster. The agent produces something plausible, someone has to untangle whether it was the right thing, and the round-trip eats the gain.

This is why the ends resist automation in a way the middle never did. The ends are where the vagueness lives. And vagueness is not a model capability problem you can wait out with the next release — it is a property of the organisation's process. No frontier model fixes an organisation that cannot say precisely what it wants.

Wall three

Individual gains don't compound

Suppose you get past the first two walls for a given engineer — clear work in, fast execution, clean output. You have made one person faster. That is a local optimisation, and local optimisations don't add up on their own.

A tool sits with the individual. It has no view of the portfolio, no opinion about whether this work is the right work, no memory that connects the research that justified a feature to the mission that built it to the operational signal that should feed the next decision. Twenty engineers each running 40% faster in twenty different directions does not give you a 40% faster organisation. It gives you twenty faster silos and a coordination problem that quietly absorbs most of the gain.

Compounding requires a layer that the individual tool cannot provide: something that allocates, sequences, connects and remembers across the whole flow. Without it, every gain is a one-time bump — which is exactly what the plateau is.

Notice that none of the three walls is about model quality. The ceiling is not waiting on a smarter model. It is structural. You have automated the easy middle and left the hard ends — and the connections between them — exactly as they were.

II.The reframe

An operating system, not more tools

If the problem is structural, the answer has to be structural. And there is a precise word for the structure that turns a pile of fast components into a system that does useful work: an operating system.

The analogy is exact, not decorative. A computer's operating system does four things. It schedules work onto scarce resources. It allocates those resources according to priority. It enforces contracts so that components can trust each other and fail safely. And it abstracts the machine so that the people writing applications don't have to think about the hardware underneath. Raw processors and memory are not a computer. The operating system is what makes them one.

Replace silicon with engineering capacity and AI agents and the mapping holds. An operating system for product development schedules work — it decides which missions run now and which wait. It allocates capacity across competing demands — experiments, new features, platform health, security. It enforces contracts — every piece of work enters in a known shape, is classified, and is routed to a workflow appropriate to its risk. And it abstracts the machine — a product manager or a researcher can put validated work into the system without needing to know how it gets built or kept alive.

The word matters because the alternatives mislead. A platform is infrastructure you build on; it is necessary but passive, and it does not decide what runs. A process is a set of rules people follow by hand; it does not allocate or remember, and it degrades the moment attention lapses. An operating system is active and autonomous within its contracts. That is the category the plateau demands, and it is the category almost no one has actually built.

Raw processors aren't a computer. Fast engineers with AI tools aren't an operating system. The OS is what turns components into a system that compounds.

III.The architecture

What the system actually looks like

Concreteness is the test. It is easy to say "operating system" and mean nothing. Here is a real architecture — one a peer organisation could adopt — built from a substrate and three planes. Each layer exists to dismantle one of the three walls.

The substrate: platform automation

Underneath everything sits a layer of autonomous agents that own the downstream end — the maintenance, security, patching and upgrade work that otherwise grows without bound. These are not editor assistants; they respond to events. A vulnerability is disclosed, an upgrade lands, an incident fires — and an agent opens the change, classifies its risk against a published rubric, and either routes it to a human verdict or, for low-risk changes, takes it through to production on its own. A code-review agent absorbs the review bottleneck that a faster middle inevitably creates. This substrate is what makes the rest deployable: it keeps the system you are building on top of healthy enough to build on.

Plane 1: insight orchestrators

The upstream end. A set of orchestrators — one each for customer success, product research, operations and platform — that continuously turn raw signals into structured, validated work. Interviews, support tickets, usage data, industry signals, incident patterns, platform risk: in at one side; enriched, specified, ready-to-build work items out the other, with a human in the loop at the validation step. This is the layer that kills vagueness at the source, so that everything downstream — including the agents — receives clear input rather than one-line tickets.

Plane 2: the portfolio orchestrator

A rich, validated backlog creates a new problem: too much worth doing. The portfolio orchestrator allocates scarce capacity across the four standing demands — experiments, new features, platform improvement, and operations and security — and holds a deliberate ratio between them rather than letting the loudest stakeholder win. This is the scheduler and the resource allocator of the operating system. It is where strategy stops being a slide and becomes an enforced allocation.

Plane 3: the mission orchestrator

Execution. Each unit of work is a mission, deliberately time-boxed to a few weeks — never a quarter. Quarterly value delivery is no longer competitive when the middle is this fast. The mission orchestrator classifies each piece of work by type and risk and the services it touches, then routes it to the appropriate structured workflow — a feature workflow, a tight-change workflow, an experiment workflow. The contract is the point: nothing executes unclassified, and classification determines how much structure and how much human oversight a mission gets.

Read the diagram top to bottom and the three walls fall in order. The substrate and Plane 1 take the two ends that tools never touched. Plane 1's structured output and Plane 3's classification remove the vagueness that blocked the ends. And the four layers, connected and sharing memory, are the coordination fabric that finally lets local speed compound into organisational leverage. That last property is the whole game, and it deserves its own metric.

IV.The metric

Leverage per engineer

You cannot manage toward a plateau you cannot see, and most engineering dashboards are built to hide this one. They measure output: commits, pull requests, story points, deploys. Output is exactly what AI tools inflated, which is why output metrics looked spectacular right up until the curve bent — and then kept looking fine while the organisation stopped improving.

The metric that exposes the plateau, and that the operating system is built to move, is leverage per engineer: the volume of customer-impacting change divided by the size of the team that produced it. Two refinements carry all the weight. Customer-impacting excludes the churn — the rework, the busywork, the changes that ship nothing a customer would notice — that output metrics happily count. And per engineer turns it into a measure of leverage rather than headcount: the question is not how much you ship, but how much each person's judgement is amplified by the system around them.

This is the number a tool can bump once and an operating system can compound. A tool raises the numerator for a while and then the ends and the coordination losses cap it. An operating system attacks every term at once: the substrate and insight orchestrators convert effort that was going to maintenance and ambiguity into customer-impacting change; the portfolio orchestrator stops capacity leaking into low-value work; the mission cadence shortens the distance between decision and impact. The destination is not a marginal lift. It is a doubling of leverage per engineer — the same people, a structurally different output — and it is reached by compounding, not by buying.

Output is what tools inflate. Leverage per engineer is what an operating system compounds. Measure the second and the plateau becomes visible.

V.The path

You don't buy an operating system — you build it

The uncomfortable implication of everything above is that there is nothing to purchase. No vendor ships your operating system, because most of it is the encoding of how your organisation decides, allocates and ships. It is built, in stages, and the stages are a maturity ladder worth naming because they tell you where you actually are.

  1. Tool adoption. Individuals use AI assistants. Output jumps. This is the stage that produces the plateau — and the stage almost everyone is stuck at, mistaking the one-time bump for a trend.
  2. Structured workflows. The organisation stops dropping agents into ambiguity. Work is specified, classified and routed through repeatable workflows. Vagueness starts to drain out of the system. This is the first stage that addresses a wall rather than the middle.
  3. Orchestrated ends. The substrate takes over downstream maintenance; insight orchestrators take over upstream enrichment. The two ends that tools never touched begin to automate. The constraint finally moves off the middle for good.
  4. Compounding. The planes connect and share memory. Allocation, execution and insight feed each other. Leverage per engineer stops being a one-time bump and starts to climb on its own. This is the operating system, fully assembled.

The diagnostic value is immediate. If your throughput jumped and then flattened, you are at stage one and you have been treating a structural problem as a shopping problem. The way up is not the next licence. It is the deliberate, unglamorous work of building stages two, three and four — and the organisations that start now will compound a lead that the ones still buying tools cannot close by buying faster.

VI.Close

The line moves

It is tempting to frame the agentic era as engineers typing faster. That framing is the plateau in disguise — it keeps your attention on the middle, on the one stretch that was never the constraint, and it sends you back to the store for another tool every time the curve bends.

The real shift is quieter and far larger. The line of responsibility between people and their machines is moving. The work that does not require human judgement — the patching, the grooming, the routine review, the maintenance that grows without end — moves to agents. What stays with people is the part that was always the point: deciding what is worth building, and judging whether what got built is right. An operating system for product development is simply the structure that moves that line deliberately, and then compounds what is left.

Tools gave you a bump. The operating system is how you build a slope. The organisations that understand the difference will spend the next few years pulling away from the ones that are still waiting for a better model to do it for them.
